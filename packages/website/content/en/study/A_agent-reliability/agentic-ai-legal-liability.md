As autonomous agents move beyond just answering questions and start taking **'Actions'** on our behalf, a scary question arises:

"If an agent messes up, who goes to jail (or pays the fine)?"

Today, we summarize the key points of **AI Legal Liability** that every engineer and business decision-maker building production agents must know.

---

### 1. Product Liability vs. Professional Negligence

In the current legal system, there are two primary ways to view an AI agent's failure.

#### Product Liability
This view suggests that the agent itself had a defect. If a model has inherent, catastrophic biases or if a 'Delete File' tool is provided without any safety rails, the **'Manufacturer (Developer)'** bears more responsibility.

#### Professional Negligence
This occurs when a user gives an unreasonable command or clicks 'Execute' without performing due diligence. If the [Human-in-the-Loop](/en/study/A_agent-reliability/human-in-the-loop-design) mechanisms discussed in Part 10 were absent, proving user negligence becomes more straightforward.

---

### 2. The 3 Main 'Crimes' of an Agent

#### Financial Errors
Accidental orders at an e-commerce site or incorrect stock trades leading to losses. While most Terms of Service state the "User is the final authority," developers struggle to avoid liability for repetitive payments caused by system errors.

#### Defamation and Misinformation
What if an agent-written blog post contains a hallucination (false information) about a real person? This can constitute defamation, and the entire process of publishing without 'human review' becomes a target for lawsuits.

#### Copyright Infringement
When an agent ignores open-source licenses and clones code, or outputs training data verbatim during a task.

---

### 3. Legal Defense Strategies for Engineers (Compliance)

How to prepare at the code level to reduce legal risk:

1. **Transparency through Logging**: The [Agent Tracing](/en/study/A_agent-reliability/agent-distributed-tracing) we covered in Part 6 acts as the 'Black Box' in court. Keep logs of all decision-making processes for at least one year.
2. **Explicit Opt-out / Approval**: Any high-risk action (payment, deletion, transmission) must be designed to require explicit user approval.
3. **Disclaimers**: Displaying "This response was generated by AI and may contain errors" in the UI is a necessary, albeit minimal, line of defense.

---

### Henry's Conclusion

AI agents evolve much faster than the law. But one thing is clear: **"An agent without logs is inherently guilty."** Accidents happen, but you must be able to prove through data whether the cause was 'uncontrollable AI whims' or 'poor engineering design.'
